What this calculator does

The Loan Calculator estimates the monthly payment and total cost of a fixed-rate loan. It uses loan amount, annual interest rate, and tenure in years to show expected monthly payment, total interest, and full repayment.

The loan amount is treated as principal, the annual rate is converted to a monthly decimal rate, and years are converted to monthly payment periods. Calculations keep precision until the interface formats monetary outputs.

When to use it

Use it for home loans, vehicle loans, education loans, equipment loans, or fixed-payment borrowing. It helps check affordability, compare rates, and see how a longer term affects total interest.

Use it for a first affordability comparison, then request the lender's full disclosure. A quoted payment may include fees, insurance, taxes, a balloon amount, or a rate structure that this fixed-payment model does not include.

Formula used

Monthly payment = P x r x (1 + r)^n / ((1 + r)^n - 1), where P is principal, r is monthly rate, and n is total monthly payments.

The formula models an amortizing balance: each payment covers the month's interest and reduces principal. With a zero rate, payment becomes principal divided by the total number of months.

Example calculation

For a 300,000 loan at 9 percent annual interest for 4 years, the estimated payment is 7,465.51 per month and total interest is about 58,344.61.

For 300,000 at 9 percent over four years, enter 300000, 9, and 4. The tool converts four years to 48 payments before evaluating the monthly formula.

Benefits

  • Estimate payment before applying
  • Compare tenures and rates
  • Understand total interest
  • Plan borrowing safely
  • Use for many fixed loans

Comparing equal principal amounts at different terms exposes the main tradeoff. More payments can lower the monthly burden while increasing the amount of time interest accrues.

How to read the result

Monthly payment is the modelled fixed instalment; total payment is that value multiplied by all months; total interest is the difference from principal. Verify payment timing and fees before using the estimate in a contract decision.

FAQs

Is this the same as EMI?

It uses similar reducing-balance logic but accepts tenure in years.

Does it include fees?

No. Add lender fees, insurance, penalties, and taxes separately.

Can interest be zero?

Yes. With zero rate, payment is principal divided by months.

Why is total interest important?

A low monthly payment can still be expensive over a long tenure.

Can I use it for credit cards?

Not usually, because credit cards use different minimum payment rules.

How an amortizing payment behaves over time

The formula chooses one payment that would reduce a fixed-rate balance to zero after the selected number of months. Interest for each period is based on the outstanding balance, so the interest share is normally larger near the beginning and the principal share grows later. This page shows lifetime totals rather than an amortization schedule, and it assumes every payment is made as planned.

A lender may quote a nominal annual rate, an annual percentage rate that incorporates certain charges, or a floating benchmark plus a margin. Those figures are not interchangeable. Confirm which rate belongs in a comparison and whether it can change. Also check origination charges, required insurance, taxes, payment timing, balloon amounts, late fees, and prepayment rules.

When testing affordability, compare more than the first monthly payment. A longer term can reduce the payment while increasing total interest. A shorter term can save interest but leave less monthly cash-flow room. Use several realistic scenarios and compare the results with the lender's written schedule before signing or refinancing.

Model checks and exclusions

  • Standard case: 100,000 at 12% for one year returns about 8,884.88 per month.
  • Zero-rate case: 24,000 over two years returns exactly 1,000 per month.
  • Rejected inputs: principal and years must be positive; annual rate cannot be negative.
  • Excluded: origination fees, insurance, taxes, variable rates, irregular payments, and prepayment charges.
