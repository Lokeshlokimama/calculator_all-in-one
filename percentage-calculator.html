What this calculator does

The Percentage Calculator finds a selected percentage of a base number. Percent means per hundred, so 15 percent means 15 parts out of 100. Percentage problems appear in exam marks, discounts, interest, taxes, profit margins, attendance, and data comparisons.

This tool answers one precise question: what is a selected percent of a base value? It supports decimals, zero, and negative numbers because the underlying multiplication remains valid for those cases.

When to use it

Use it while shopping, checking discounts, calculating tax portions, comparing salary changes, finding marks, estimating tips, reviewing business reports, or solving school math.

Do not confuse “18 percent of 1,200” with “increase 1,200 by 18 percent.” The first returns 216; the second requires adding that 216 to the base and returns 1,416.

Formula used

Result = (percentage / 100) x base value.

Dividing by 100 converts percent to decimal form: 18 percent becomes 0.18. Multiplying 0.18 by the base then gives the requested portion in the same unit as the base.

Step-by-step example

To find 18 percent of 1,200, calculate 18 / 100 x 1,200 = 216. If that is tax, the total after adding tax is 1,416.

For a 20 percent discount on 750, the calculator returns 150. Subtract 150 from 750 to obtain the discounted price of 600; the calculator's result is the portion, not the final price.

Benefits

  • Solve routine percent math
  • Check discounts
  • Understand tax portions
  • Avoid report mistakes
  • Use one formula widely

Keep the base unit consistent. If the base is rupees, kilograms, marks, or survey responses, the result has that same unit; only the percentage input is unitless.

How to read the result

A negative result is expected when the base and percentage have opposite signs. A zero base always gives zero. Decimal display is rounded to two places, while the multiplication itself uses the browser's full numeric precision.

Common mistakes

  • Confusing “percent of” with percentage change.
  • Using the new value instead of the original value as the change base.
  • Forgetting that 18 percent means 0.18 in multiplication.
  • Rounding intermediate values too early.

FAQs

What does percent mean?

Percent means per hundred. A value of 25 percent means 25 out of every 100.

Can I use decimals?

Yes. Decimal percentages such as 2.5 percent work for interest, tax, or small changes.

How do I add a percentage?

Find the percentage amount, then add it to the base number.

How do I subtract a discount?

Find the discount amount, then subtract it from the original price.

Is this useful for GST?

Yes for simple math, but use the GST calculator for inclusive and exclusive tax breakdowns.

Three percentage questions that look similar

This page calculates “what is p percent of x?” by multiplying x by p divided by 100. Other common questions need a different setup. To ask “a is what percent of b?”, divide a by b and multiply by 100. To ask “what is the percentage change from old to new?”, subtract old from new, divide by old, and multiply by 100. The sign then indicates an increase or decrease.

Reverse percentages need special care. If a price after an 18-percent increase is 1,180, the original is not 1,180 minus 18 percent of 1,180. Divide 1,180 by 1.18 to recover 1,000. The same issue appears with tax-inclusive prices, markups, and compounded changes because the percentage is applied to a particular base.

Always name the base before calculating. “Sales grew by 10 percent” is incomplete without the earlier sales value, and “10 percentage points” is different from “10 percent.” Moving from 20 percent to 30 percent is a rise of 10 percentage points but a 50-percent relative increase.

Worked checks and interpretation

  • Standard case: 18% of 1,200 equals 216.
  • Zero case: any finite percent of zero equals zero.
  • Signed case: 10% of -50 equals -5, which is useful when modelling a decrease or loss.
  • Decimal case: 2.5% of 80 equals 2; enter 2.5 rather than 0.025 in the percentage field.
