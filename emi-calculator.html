What this calculator does

The EMI Calculator estimates the fixed monthly payment required to repay a loan over a selected tenure. EMI stands for Equated Monthly Instalment. It combines a portion of principal and a portion of interest charged by the lender, then shows a simple monthly figure that is easier to budget around.

The calculation runs in your browser and retains full precision before rounding the three displayed totals to two decimal places. It is designed for fixed-rate, reducing-balance loans with one payment every month.

When to use it

Use it before applying for a home loan, car loan, personal loan, education loan, or business loan. It helps compare offers, test shorter or longer tenures, and understand whether a monthly instalment fits your income.

Compare the estimate with the lender's repayment schedule and annual percentage rate. Processing fees, insurance, taxes, prepayments, late fees, and a variable interest rate are outside this formula and can change the actual borrowing cost.

Formula used

EMI = P x r x (1 + r)^n / ((1 + r)^n - 1), where P is principal, r is monthly interest rate, and n is number of monthly instalments.

The annual percentage entered is divided by 12 and then by 100 to obtain r. When the rate is zero, the calculator avoids division by zero and uses principal divided by the number of payments.

Step-by-step example

If you borrow 500,000 at 10 percent annual interest for 5 years, n is 60 and the estimated EMI is about 10,624 per month. Total repayment is about 637,440, so total interest is about 137,440.

To reproduce it, enter 500000, 10, and 60. Entering 10 as the rate means ten percent per year—not 0.10 percent and not ten percent per month.

Benefits

  • Compare loan offers
  • Plan monthly budgets
  • Estimate total interest
  • Test tenure changes
  • Check affordability before applying

Change one input at a time to see the tradeoff: a longer tenure usually lowers EMI but raises total interest, while a lower rate reduces both payment and interest when all other terms stay equal.

How to read the result

Monthly EMI is the modelled periodic payment. Total interest is total repayment minus principal, and total payable is EMI multiplied by the number of months. A bank quote can differ because of day-count rules, payment dates, rate changes, fees, or rounding at each instalment.

Common mistakes

  • Entering the annual interest rate as a monthly rate.
  • Using years where the tenure field expects months.
  • Comparing only the EMI and ignoring total interest and lender fees.
  • Treating the estimate as a binding bank quotation.

FAQs

What does EMI mean?

EMI means Equated Monthly Instalment, the fixed monthly amount paid to repay a loan over time.

Does EMI include processing fees?

No. This calculator estimates principal and interest only. Lender fees, insurance, taxes, or penalties may change the final cost.

Why does a longer tenure reduce EMI?

A longer tenure spreads repayment across more months, lowering the monthly payment but usually increasing total interest.

Can I use this for a home loan?

Yes. It works for standard reducing-balance EMI style loans, including home, car, personal, and education loans.

Is the result exact?

It is a planning estimate. Your lender may round values differently or use a different schedule.

What the EMI total does—and does not—show

A fixed EMI does not mean the principal and interest portions are equal every month. Early in a reducing-balance schedule, interest is calculated on a larger outstanding principal, so more of the payment usually goes to interest. As the balance falls, the interest portion falls and more of the same EMI reduces principal. This page reports the payment and lifetime totals, not a month-by-month amortization table.

When comparing two offers, use the same principal and tenure, then compare both EMI and total interest. A lower monthly amount can result from a longer term rather than a cheaper loan. Also compare the lender's annual percentage rate, processing charge, insurance, taxes, prepayment terms, and whether the quoted rate is fixed or floating. Those items can make an offer with a similar EMI more expensive or more risky.

For budgeting, leave room above the calculated EMI for other debt, essential expenses, and rate changes. The output is a mathematical scenario—not an affordability approval or a promise that a lender will use exactly the same day-count, rounding, or payment date.

Assumptions, test cases, and source

  • Standard case: 100,000 at 12% for 12 months returns 8,884.88 per month before display-currency formatting.
  • Zero-rate case: 12,000 across 12 months returns exactly 1,000 per month.
  • Rejected inputs: principal and tenure must be greater than zero; interest cannot be negative.
  • Model boundary: payments are treated as end-of-month and the rate stays constant for the full term.

Formula reference: Microsoft Support—PMT function, which also notes that payment calculations exclude taxes and many loan fees.
