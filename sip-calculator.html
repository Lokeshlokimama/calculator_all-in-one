What this calculator does

The SIP Calculator estimates the future value of a monthly investment made through a Systematic Investment Plan. SIPs are commonly used for mutual fund investing because they let investors contribute a fixed amount every month.

The projection compounds a fixed hypothetical annual return monthly and assumes the same contribution at the beginning of every month. It separates money contributed from modelled growth so the assumption is visible.

When to use it

Use it for long-term goals such as education, a home down payment, retirement, travel, or wealth building. It helps test whether a monthly investment is enough for a target and how duration affects returns.

Use it to compare contribution and duration scenarios, not to predict a fund's actual outcome. Market returns vary, losses are possible, and fees, taxes, skipped payments, and fund-specific timing can materially change the result.

Formula used

Future value = M x (((1 + r)^n - 1) / r) x (1 + r), where M is monthly investment, r is monthly return, and n is total months.

The annual return is divided by 12 and 100 to obtain the monthly rate. The final multiplication by 1 + r represents beginning-of-month contributions; an end-of-month model would be slightly lower.

Example calculation

If you invest 5,000 monthly for 10 years at 12 percent annual return, projected value is about 1,161,695, invested amount is 600,000, and estimated return is about 561,695.

At 5,000 per month for ten years, contributions total 600,000. A constant 12-percent annual assumption produces about 1,161,695, of which about 561,695 is modelled growth.

Benefits

  • Estimate maturity value
  • Compare return assumptions
  • Separate invested amount and gains
  • Plan goals monthly
  • Understand compounding

Duration has a nonlinear effect because earlier contributions compound for longer. Compare a conservative range of return assumptions instead of treating one optimistic percentage as a forecast.

How to read the result

Invested amount is simply contribution times months. Estimated returns are maturity minus invested amount. The maturity figure is nominal and does not adjust for inflation, volatility, fees, taxes, or the risk of loss.

FAQs

Does SIP guarantee returns?

No. SIP returns depend on market performance and are not guaranteed.

What return rate should I enter?

Use a realistic annual expected return based on asset type and risk.

Can I use this for RD?

It is designed for SIP-style compounding. RD rules may differ.

Why does duration matter?

Longer duration gives compounding more time to work.

Is this investment advice?

No. It is an educational estimate. Consult a financial advisor before investing.

Why return assumptions change the projection so much

Compounding means the assumed return affects earlier contributions for longer than later contributions. Over a long period, a small change in the entered annual rate can create a large difference in the projected maturity value. That sensitivity is a reason to compare conservative, middle, and optimistic scenarios—not evidence that the highest input is a likely outcome.

The model uses a smooth monthly rate derived from one annual percentage. Actual market returns arrive unevenly, can be negative, and are affected by the order of gains and losses. Fund expenses, taxes, tracking difference, skipped contributions, contribution-date changes, withdrawals, and inflation are not deducted. A real account can therefore finish above or below the projection even when its long-term average return appears similar.

Focus first on controllable inputs: contribution amount, duration, regularity, costs, diversification, and risk appropriate to the goal. Use the estimate to understand a scenario and to ask better questions. It is not a guaranteed return, fund recommendation, target-date promise, or substitute for regulated investment advice.

Projection assumptions and test cases

  • Standard case: 5,000 monthly, 12% annual return, and 10 years produces about 1,161,695.38.
  • Zero-return case: 1,000 monthly for one year remains 12,000.
  • Timing: the formula assumes contributions occur at the beginning of each month.
  • Not modelled: changing returns, fund expenses, taxes, inflation, missed contributions, and withdrawals.
